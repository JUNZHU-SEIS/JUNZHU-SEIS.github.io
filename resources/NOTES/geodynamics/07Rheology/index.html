    Faults and Rock Rheology

    Elastic, viscous and plastic behavior in the Earth's interior

    Terminology

    1. Rheology: the study of the flow of the matter, primarily in a liquid or gas state, but also as "soft solids" or solids under conditions in which they respond with plastic flow rather than deforming elastically in response to applied force.
    2. Newtonian fluid: a fluid in which the viscous stresses arising from its flow, at every point, are linearly correlated to the local strain rate--the rate of change of its deformation over time. That is equivalent to saying those forces are proportional to the rates of change of the fluids's velocity vector as one moves away from the point in question in various directions.
    3. dextral: on the right side or the right hand. For sinistral, vice versa.

    Rock failure and faults

    1. rocks are brittle at atmosphere pressure and room temperature
    2. elastic behavior until faults forms
    3. faults are introduced when the shear stress is larger than the yield stress. The yield stress increases with the normal stress (Byerlee's law).

    Fault types

    1. Normal faults: \(\sigma_{yy}=\rho gy, \sigma_{xx}=\rho gy +\Delta \sigma_{xx}, \sigma_{zz}=\rho gy +\nu \Delta \sigma_{xx}\) (due to \(\epsilon_{zz}=0\)), where \(\sigma_{xx}\lt0\)
    2. Thrust faults \: the same as Normal fault, but \(\sigma_{xx}>0\)
    3. strike-slip faults:

    Byerlee's law

    It concerns the shear stress required to slide one rock over another. The rocks have macroscopically flat surfaces, but the surfaces have small asperities that make them "rough". For a given experiment and at normal stresses \(\sigma_n\) below about 2000 bars (200 Mpa) the shear stress increases approximately linearly with the normal stress (\(\tau=0.85\sigma_n\)) and is highly dependent on rock type and the character (roughness) of the surfaces. Byerlee's law states that with increased normal stress the required shear stress continues to increase, but the rate of increase decreases (\(\tau=0.5+0.6\sigma_n\)), and becomes nearly independent of rock type.

    Mohr-coulomb yielding criterion

    \(\tau=\sigma \tan (\phi)+c\)
    \(\phi\) is called the angle of internal friction, it's a intrinsic property of the rocks
    \(c\) is cohesion
    For wet rock with a pore pressure (neglecting cohesion), \(|\tau|=f_s(\sigma_n-p_w)\)
    In the context of hydraulic fracturing in shale gas mining, defining an effective coefficient of friction.

    Anderson's theory of faulting

    Anderson recognized that since the principal stress directions are directions of zero shear stress, we can place faults in the context of principal stress. All faults have a common function, to extend the crust in one direction and shorten it in another. The directions of shortening and stretching are at right angles to one another.

    For a thrust faulting (assumed with dip angle \(\beta\)), we get \(\Delta \sigma_{xx}\propto \frac{1}{\pm \sin(2\theta)-f_s(1+cos(2\theta))}\), where \(\theta=\pi/2-\beta\). Faulting will occur at angles that minimize \(|\Delta \sigma_{xx}|\),
    so \(\tan(2\beta) = \pm \frac{1}{f_s}\) & \(\Delta \sigma_{xx}=\frac{\pm 2f_s(\rho gy-p_w)}{(1+f_s^2)^{1/2}\mp f_s}\)

    Evidence of the viscous layering in the Earth's mantle

    1. long-wavelength geoid anomalies
    2. mantle convection wavelength
    3. inversion from post-glacial rebound: good for two-layer model, trade-off for four-layer model
